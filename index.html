🧩 Problem Framing

Customers, users, and field teams naturally describe problems in solution language — "I need a button," "we need a faster process." Problem framing is the discipline of excavating the real need from the surface request — producing a problem statement with zero solution language.

The test: A clean problem statement contains no words that describe a solution, feature, technology, or approach. If you can read it and still have multiple ways to solve it — you have found the problem.

Request or input I've received: [paste the raw request, ticket, or ask]

Please:
1. Identify all the solution language in this request
2. Rewrite it as a pure problem statement — no solution language of any kind
3. Tell me what we still don't know about the problem

The output should describe a human need, not a product response.

→ PMs and field teams work from shared problem statements, not competing solution proposals.

📊 Prioritization

PMs often make tradeoffs implicitly or inconsistently. This framework gives the team a shared language without over-engineering the decision.

Dealbreaker first: Committed customer promise, exec mandate, or contractual obligation → goes to the top, no scoring needed.

Everything else: Reach × Impact × Frequency (score each 4→1). Set product priority before asking engineering for sizing.

Initiatives to prioritize: [list your initiatives]
Context: [company stage, current priorities, constraints]

Score each using:
- Dealbreaker? (if yes, goes to the top)
- Reach: all/most (4) · significant segment (3) · specific subset (2) · edge cases (1)
- Impact: blocker (4) · hard workaround (3) · reasonable workaround (2) · nice to have (1)
- Frequency: daily (4) · weekly (3) · monthly (2) · rare (1)

Calculate Reach × Impact × Frequency. Then rank, flag assumptions,
highlight anything misleading, and note what to stop or defer.

→ Explicit, consistent prioritization. Stakeholders understand the reasoning, not just the ranking.

Session 1 — Foundation

Problem framing · Initiative naming · Prioritization · Breakdown

Good product work starts before the first line of code — and before the first solution is proposed. This session builds the habit of getting from a solution-shaped request to the real underlying need, then turning that into an initiative that is clearly named, properly ranked, and scoped to ship.

Outcomes

  • Recognise solution language in requests from customers, field teams, and stakeholders
  • Excavate the real underlying need — producing a problem statement with zero solution language
  • Name initiatives in a way that communicates value, not just activity
  • Score and rank work using a shared framework — without over-engineering the decision
  • Scope an MVP that tests the core assumption without building everything

Session Structure

Pre-Work

  • One real request, ticket, or ask that came to you recently — ideally one that arrived in solution language ("we need X," "can you add Y")
  • A rough sense of 3–4 things currently on your backlog or roadmap

Warm-Up (10 min)

What is the most solution-shaped request you have received recently? What was the actual ask underneath it — and did you ever find out?

Listen for: feature requests with no stated problem, customer asks that jump straight to implementation, internal escalations that describe a fix rather than a need.

Core Concept 1: Problem Framing

People — customers, field teams, stakeholders — naturally describe problems in solution language. They tell you what they want, not why they want it.

"I want a button." "We need a faster process." "Can you add a filter?"

These are solution statements dressed up as problems. Until you strip the solution out, you cannot tell whether you are building the right thing, the wrong thing, or something that already exists.

Problem framing is the discipline of excavating the real need from the surface request. It is not a software skill — it is a thinking skill. The method is the same for a software request, a process complaint, a customer escalation, or a management ask.

The Excavation Move

When you receive a solution-shaped request, ask: "What will that give you?" Keep asking until the answer no longer contains any solution language. That is the problem.

RequestAskDig deeperProblem
"I want a dashboard"What will a dashboard give you?"I'll be able to see if things are working"People have no way to know if things are working until something breaks
"We need a CSV export"What will exporting give you?"I can put it in our reporting tool"Teams cannot get data into their existing reporting workflow without manual steps
"I want a faster horse"What will a faster horse give you?"I can cover more distance per day"Travellers cannot cover long distances in a day under their own power

The Clean Problem Statement Test

Does this sentence contain any words that describe a solution, feature, technology, or approach? If yes — dig one level deeper. A clean problem statement should leave multiple possible solutions on the table.

Warm-up (5 min, whole group): Identify the solution language in each of these:

- "Customers want export to CSV"
- "We need a dashboard"
- "Users are confused in onboarding"
- "We need faster ingestion"
- "Sales needs a button to resend the welcome email"

Main exercise (15 min): Use your real request from pre-work.

  1. Write the request exactly as it came to you — solution language and all
  2. Ask "what will that give you?" at least twice. Stop when no solution language remains.
  3. Run it through Claude:

Please:
1. Identify all the solution language in this request
2. Rewrite it as a pure problem statement — no solution language of any kind
3. Tell me what we still don't know about the problem

The output should describe a human need, not a product response to that need.

Compare your hand-excavated version with Claude's. Where do they differ? What solution language did you miss?

Core Concept 2: Initiative Naming

The name of an initiative is the first piece of communication. Naming only works if you have a clean problem statement to name from. This is why problem framing comes first — the output of Exercise 1 is the input to Exercise 2.

Format: Who / What / Outcome

PartQuestion it answers
WhoWho is this for?
WhatWhat are we changing or enabling?
OutcomeWhat improves as a result?

Use the clean problem statement from Exercise 1 — not the original request.

For each name: state it, explain what it emphasises, explain what it de-emphasises.

Core Concept 3: Prioritization

Step 1 — Check for a dealbreaker. Committed customer promise, exec mandate, or contractual obligation? It goes to the top. Dealbreakers bypass the framework.

Step 2 — Score everything else on three dimensions (pick one option per row, no half-scores):

Dimension4321
ReachAll/most customersSignificant segmentSpecific subsetEdge cases
ImpactBlockerHard workaroundReasonable workaroundNice to have
FrequencyDailyWeeklyMonthlyRare

Score = Reach × Impact × Frequency (range: 1–64)

Step 3 — Set product priority before sizing. Have a conviction about what matters before asking engineering how long it takes.

Engineering sizing (order of magnitude only): ~2 days · ~2 weeks · ~2 months

Exercise 3 — Prioritization (20 min)

Score 3–4 real items from your backlog, then run them through Claude:

Initiatives to prioritize: [list your initiatives]
Context: [company stage, current priorities, constraints]

For each initiative:
- Dealbreaker? (customer commitment / exec mandate / contractual)
- Reach: all/most (4) · significant segment (3) · specific subset (2) · edge cases (1)
- Impact: blocker (4) · hard workaround (3) · reasonable workaround (2) · nice to have (1)
- Frequency: daily (4) · weekly (3) · monthly (2) · rare (1)

Multiply: Reach × Impact × Frequency = product priority score

Then rank, flag the key assumption behind each impact score,
highlight anything where the score feels misleading, and note what to stop or defer.

Core Concept 4: Breakdown + MVP Scoping

The MVP question is not "what is the minimum version?" — it is "what is the smallest version that tests our core assumption?"

Exercise 4 — Breakdown + MVP Scoping (20 min)

Use your top-ranked initiative from Exercise 3.

Initiative: [name and one-sentence description]
Goal: [what outcome does this drive?]

Break this down into:
1. Key components or workstreams
2. Epics (2–5 sentence descriptions of major deliverables)
3. User stories for the first epic (as a... I want... so that...)
4. Dependencies — what must be true before each component can start?
5. Delivery risks
6. Recommended MVP scope — smallest version that tests the core assumption

Flag anything that looks like scope creep.

Key Insight

Solution language is everywhere. Learning to hear it is the first skill. A clean problem statement leaves multiple solutions on the table. Named initiatives get aligned faster. Scored work stops the loudest voice winning. Scoped MVPs ship.

Session 2 — Landscape

Competitive analysis · Strategy translation

Strong product strategy comes from understanding not just what competitors build — but how they think and where they are going. And from connecting that understanding to the decisions you make every week.

Outcomes

  • Move beyond feature comparison to strategic competitive understanding
  • Identify where you are strong, where you are vulnerable, and what to do about it
  • Connect competitive and market signals directly to roadmap decisions
  • Use strategy translation to align daily decisions with company direction

Session Structure

TimeActivity
0:00 – 0:10Warm-up
0:10 – 0:25Concept: Competitive analysis
0:25 – 0:40Exercise 1: From signals to insight
0:40 – 0:50Concept + Exercise 2: Strategy translation
0:50 – 1:00Debrief

Pre-Work

  • 1–2 key competitors relevant to your product area
  • Basic familiarity with what they do and where they come up in deals
  • Optional: examples of recent wins/losses involving that competitor

Warm-Up (10 min)

When did a competitor last change something you were planning to do — or something you thought was safe? What did you learn from it?

Core Concept: Competitive Analysis

Competitive analysis is not a feature checklist, a list of "they're better/worse," or a static document. It is a way to understand how competitors win, where they are vulnerable, and what that means for us.

Instead ofThink
"They have better dashboards""They win with business users due to strong reporting — which makes us weaker in executive visibility, but stronger in technical depth"

In AI products, your competition is not only other vendors — it is also the customer's ability to build a solution themselves. Ask: could a customer replicate this with off-the-shelf AI tools? Where would a self-built solution fall short?

Practical tip: Each PM owns 1–2 competitors. Maintains a living view, not a static document.

Exercise 1 — From Signals to Insight (30 min)

Competitor: [name] · Our product: [description] · Context: [what decision is this for?]

Analyse:
1. Where are they stronger than us, and why?
2. Where are we stronger, and is that defensible?
3. Their likely strategic positioning and target customer
4. Where are we most vulnerable?
5. What opportunities does this create for us?

Focus on strategic implications, not feature lists.

Upgrade: What assumptions are we making that might be wrong?
What would need to be true for them to become a major threat?

Core Concept: Strategy Translation

Most PMs can recite the company strategy. Fewer can explain how today's prioritization decision connects to it. Without active translation, teams drift — defaulting to whatever is loudest.

Before any significant decision, ask: which part of our strategy does this serve? If this initiative does not exist, does anything in our strategy break?

Exercise 2 — Strategy Translation (10 min)

Company/product strategy: [paste strategy statement or key pillars]
Initiatives I'm considering: [list them]

How does each initiative support (or not) the strategy?
What should we prioritise more or less?
Where are we drifting without realising it?
What's conspicuously missing given our stated direction?

Debrief question: What would we stop doing if we took our strategy literally?

Key Insights

Competitive analysis is only valuable if it changes what we do. Signals are easy to collect — insight comes from connecting them. Strategy translation is not a quarterly exercise — it is a decision-making habit.

Session 3 — Customers

Customer conversations · Customer insight synthesis

The shortest path from customer conversation to product decision is the one most PMs skip. This session compresses the research loop — from how to enter a conversation to how to extract a sharp insight that drives a decision.

Session Structure

TimeActivity
0:00 – 0:10Warm-up
0:10 – 0:25Concept + Exercise 1: Conversation prep
0:25 – 0:40Concept + Exercise 2: Insight synthesis
0:40 – 0:55Group debrief
0:55 – 1:00Wrap

Warm-Up (10 min)

Share a time you left a customer call without a clear insight. What happened next? What did you do with the notes?

Core Concept: Customer Conversations

Failure mode 1 — The feature review: The PM walks through the roadmap asking "does this seem useful?" The customer says yes to everything. Nothing is learned.

Failure mode 2 — The leading question: "Would it help if we made the export faster?" The customer says yes. The PM hears validation. The real problem was never surfaced.

The most useful question you probably don't ask: "Walk me through the last time you tried to do [thing] and it didn't go the way you expected." This gets a story. Stories contain the problems direct questions never surface.

Exercise 1 — Conversation Prep (15 min)

Customer context: [role, company, how long a customer, known pain points]
What we want to learn: [question or hypothesis]
Relevant products/features: [brief list]

Give me:
1. Five open-ended questions that surface real problems (not validate assumptions)
2. Three hypotheses I have going in
3. Signals to listen for — what would confirm or challenge each hypothesis?
4. What to avoid asking
5. One provocative question I'd normally skip

Core Concept: Customer Insight Synthesis

Synthesis is not summarising what the customer said. It is answering: what does this mean for what we build?

Raw noteSynthesised insight
"She exports to Excel every Friday"Operations users maintain a manual weekly reporting workflow because our data can't be consumed directly by their BI tools — a recurring time cost, not a one-off request
"He said onboarding took longer than expected"New customers hit an undisclosed complexity gap between the sales demo and production setup — likely causing early churn risk

Customers are good at describing pain. They are not always good at diagnosing cause. "We need a dashboard" is a request. "We cannot tell if the system is working until something breaks" is the problem.

Exercise 2 — Insight Synthesis (15 min)

Use notes from a past customer conversation.

Customer call notes: [paste raw notes — messy is fine]
Context: [product area, what were we exploring?]

Extract:
1. Key insights — what did we learn? (what it means, not what they said)
2. Patterns — themes across multiple things they said
3. Real problems vs stated requests
4. Surprises — what challenged our assumptions?
5. Opportunities — what should we explore or build?

Be specific. Don't restate — interpret.

Key Insights

The best customer questions are the ones that surface problems you weren't expecting to hear. Synthesis is not summarising — it's extracting meaning. AI structures the analysis. Product judgment decides what to do with it.

Wrap

"Before my next customer conversation, I will spend [X minutes] preparing with Claude. After the call, I will run the synthesis prompt within [X hours]."

Session 4 — Alignment

Decision framing · Selling & defending decisions

Making a good decision is half the job. Getting it aligned is the other half. This session covers both: how to frame a decision so it is clear and defensible, and how to present it so stakeholders understand the reasoning rather than just the conclusion.

Session Structure

TimeActivity
0:00 – 0:10Warm-up
0:10 – 0:25Concept + Exercise 1: Decision framing
0:25 – 0:45Concept + Exercise 2: Selling and defending
0:45 – 0:55Group debrief
0:55 – 1:00Wrap

Warm-Up (10 min)

Share a decision that was right but didn't land well. What made it hard to sell? What would you do differently?

Core Concept: Decision Framing

Most product decisions are communicated as conclusions: "We decided to do X." This skips the reasoning — which means stakeholders can't evaluate whether it makes sense, can't flag missing information, and can't tell what would change the outcome.

The "optimising for" step is the most skipped. Two people can agree on the facts and disagree on the decision because they're optimising for different things — speed vs quality, growth vs retention. Making the optimisation criteria explicit converts a values debate into a tradeoffs conversation.

Exercise 1 — Decision Framing (15 min)

Decision I need to make: [describe it]
Context: [relevant background, constraints, timeline]

Frame with:
1. The core question we're actually deciding
2. Options on the table (including "do nothing")
3. Pros and cons of each option
4. What we're optimising for (and explicitly not optimising for)
5. Recommendation and the key assumption it rests on
6. What would change the recommendation

Core Concept: Selling & Defending Decisions

The goal is explanation, not defence. When you explain well, most reasonable pushback either reveals missing information you should incorporate, or reveals a values disagreement that can be named and resolved.

Three types of objections:

  • "I have information you don't" → take it seriously, update if warranted
  • "I'm optimising for something different" → name the difference, have the values conversation
  • "I don't trust the process" → the issue is the relationship, not the decision — address separately

Exercise 2 — Selling & Defending (20 min)

Part A — Prepare (10 min):

Decision I need to align on: [what you decided]
Key objections I expect: [list]
Stakeholders: [who they are and what they care about]

Help me present:
1. Context — why this decision exists
2. Options considered and why ruled out
3. Tradeoffs in our chosen approach
4. Recommendation, stated with confidence
5. How to handle each expected objection — direct, non-defensive

Tone: clear and confident, not defensive.

Part B — Role play (10 min): In pairs, one person presents, one raises objections. Switch. The second person should raise an objection you didn't prepare for.

Key Insights

Clear decisions improve alignment. The "optimising for" question is where most alignment failures happen — name it early. Objections are information. Know which type you're facing before you respond.

Zoom B — Risk & Success

Risk identification · Metrics & success criteria

The two questions most PMs skip at the start of an initiative: what could go wrong, and how will we know if it worked? This session builds the habit of answering both before you start building.

Session Structure

TimeActivity
0:00 – 0:05Setup — each person picks a real initiative
0:05 – 0:15Exercise 1: Risk identification
0:15 – 0:25Exercise 2: Metrics and success criteria
0:25 – 0:30Shareback + wrap

Pre-Work

Bring one initiative in planning or early execution — something where you haven't yet explicitly defined risks or success metrics.

Exercise 1 — Risk Identification (10 min)

Initiative or decision: [describe what you're working on]

Identify risks across three dimensions:
1. Product risks — will customers use it, want it, be able to use it?
2. Technical risks — can we build it, at what cost, with what dependencies?
3. Adoption and GTM risks — will it reach the right people and get used?

Highlight the top 3 risks to address before proceeding.
What are we most likely missing?

Goal: [business or user outcome]

Define:
1. Leading indicators — early signals we're on track (measurable within weeks)
2. Lagging indicators — outcomes we care about (measurable within months)
3. What quantitative success looks like — be specific
4. What would tell us to iterate or stop
5. How we'd measure each in practice

Review together: Are your leading indicators early enough to act on? Do your success metrics connect to a decision?

Key Insights

Risks identified late are expensive. A success metric that doesn't connect to a decision is just a number — define what you'll do with it before you measure it.

● Self-paced · Use mid-week on real work

Day-to-Day Skills

Save this page. Reach for these prompts when you need them.

⏳ Time Prioritization

When to use it: Your week is full and you're not sure you're working on the right things.

My tasks and meetings this week: [paste your list]

1. Classify each item: urgent + important / important not urgent / urgent not important / neither
2. What should I NOT do this week — and why?
3. What should I delegate or delay, and to whom or when?
4. If I could only do three things this week that create real impact, what are they?

Be direct. My goal is to protect time for high-leverage work.

→ The week ends with something shipped or decided — not just attended.

🗞 Weekly Product Narrative

When to use it: You need to send a weekly update. Takes ten minutes and replaces a 30-minute status meeting.

This week:
- What moved forward: [list]
- What is stuck or blocked: [list]
- Key decisions made: [list]
- What I'm concerned about: [list]
- Audience: [team / leadership / stakeholders]

Write a weekly product narrative that:
1. Leads with what matters most (not chronology)
2. Is honest about what's stuck and why
3. Flags the one thing that needs attention or a decision
4. Ends with what we're focused on next week

Under 200 words. No jargon.

🧪 Prototyping (Thinking Through Building)

When to use it: Before writing requirements or briefing design — when you have an idea but haven't mapped the flow.

Idea or feature: [describe what you want to build]
User: [who is this for?]
Job to be done: [what are they trying to accomplish?]

Before we build anything:
1. Walk through the user flow step by step
2. What are the edge cases?
3. What could break or frustrate users?
4. What's the simplest version to test the core assumption?
5. What assumption are we most at risk of getting wrong?

📝 Requirements Writing

When to use it: Before handing a ticket to engineering — to reduce back-and-forth.

Requirement or user story: [paste your current wording]
Context: [what is this part of? what does engineering need to know?]

Rewrite this to be:
1. Clear — one unambiguous reading only
2. Testable — you could write an acceptance test from it
3. Appropriately scoped — not too broad, not too narrow
4. Free of implementation detail — describes what, not how

Then flag missing edge cases or acceptance criteria.

🚀 Release Preparation

When to use it: Before any release that touches customers or requires cross-team communication.

What is shipping: [list tickets, features, or fixes]
Audience: [internal team / customers / sales / leadership]

Prepare:
1. Release notes — customer language, benefit-first, no ticket numbers
2. Internal summary — what the team needs to know
3. Top 3 post-launch risks and how we're monitoring them
4. Potential customer confusion — what might they misunderstand?
5. Sales enablement points — 2–3 things sales should know

Sales Collateral

Product-level assets maintained by PMs · Initiative-level assets created per launch

PMs are best-placed to arm the field — they understand the product, the positioning, and the problems it solves. Without PM-authored collateral, sales and field teams improvise, overclaim, or undersell. With it, every customer conversation starts from the same accurate foundation.

AssetLevelUse
Product one-pagerProductEarly-stage conversations, leave-behind
Competitive battlecardProductWhen a specific competitor comes up in a deal
Conversation guideProductDiscovery questions, objection responses, what to avoid
Launch briefingInitiativeField enablement at the point of release
Initiative one-pagerInitiativeExternal-facing summary for prospects and customers

Product-Level Assets

These are living documents. A PM owns each one and keeps it current. Update trigger: positioning shift, major new capability, ICP change.

1. Product One-Pager

A single page that explains what the product does, who it is for, and why it wins. Used in early-stage conversations and as a leave-behind.

Product: [name and one-sentence description]
Target customer: [role, company type, size]
Core problem we solve: [in customer language, no solution words]
Main capabilities: [list]
Key differentiators: [what makes us hard to replace]
Customer proof points: [outcomes, metrics, quotes]

Draft a product one-pager with:
1. A plain-language problem statement (no solution language)
2. What the product does, from the customer's perspective
3. A who-it's-for table (role / what they care about / what they get)
4. Three durable reasons we win
5. One or two specific proof points

One page. No jargon. No feature lists.

2. Competitive Battlecard

A quick-reference card for the field when a specific competitor comes up. One card per competitor. Be honest — the field needs accurate information, not cheerleading.

Competitor: [name] · Our product: [brief description]
Context: [what deals do they show up in? what stage?]

Draft a competitive battlecard with:
1. How they position (1–2 sentences)
2. Where they genuinely win (be honest)
3. Where we win (be specific)
4. A table of their likely objections about us — with direct, confident responses
5. A table of our strongest claims against them — with supporting evidence
6. One memorable sentence capturing why we beat them

Do not make us sound better than we are.

3. Conversation Guide

A reference for the field covering common questions, objections, and conversations. Not a script — a thinking tool.

Product: [name and description]
Common situations the field encounters: [list]
Objections that come up repeatedly: [list]
Discovery questions that have worked: [list if known]
Things to avoid saying: [list if known]

Draft a conversation guide with:
1. One opening question that surfaces the real problem
2. A table of common situations — what they mean, how to respond
3. Five discovery questions that surface the problems we solve
4. Q&A for the most common questions
5. Two or three things to avoid saying and why

Reference tool, not a script. Practical and direct.

Initiative-Level Assets

Create these per launch. They sit on top of the product-level assets and explain what is new.

4. Launch Briefing (for the field)

What the field needs to know when something new ships. Focused on the conversation it enables, not the feature itself. Readable in two minutes.

Initiative: [name and one-sentence description]
What is shipping: [list the capabilities or changes]
Who benefits most: [customer segments, roles]
Problem it solves: [customer language — no solution words]
What customers might ask: [anticipated questions]
What not to overclaim: [known limitations or caveats]

Draft a field briefing with:
1. What changed — plain language, customer's perspective
2. Who to talk to and why
3. A suggested conversation opener
4. Q&A for likely questions
5. One or two things not to say or overclaim

One page. Readable in two minutes.

5. Initiative One-Pager (external)

A short external-facing summary of a new capability. Used in follow-up emails, proposals, and customer conversations.

Initiative: [name]
Problem it solves: [customer language, no solution words]
What we built: [plain language description]
Who benefits: [role, team, segment]
Concrete outcome: [what changes — be specific]
Proof point if available: [metric, customer story]
Next step for the customer: [what should they do?]

Draft an external one-pager with:
1. The problem (customer language, no solution words)
2. What we built (plain language, customer's perspective)
3. What changes for them (specific and concrete)
4. Who it is for
5. How to get started

No jargon. Readable in under a minute.
